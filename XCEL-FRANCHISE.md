# Xcel Energy Franchise Agreement with Denver

*Sources: Denverite (Andrew Kenney, Aug. 27, 2025); denvergov.org*

## What It Is

The franchise agreement grants Xcel Energy (Public Service Company of Colorado) — a state-regulated monopoly utility — the non-exclusive right to use Denver's public rights-of-way (streets, alleys, airport property) to build and maintain infrastructure delivering electricity, natural gas, steam, and chilled water. Xcel also owns and operates Denver's streetlights under the agreement. Denver voters have been approving these agreements every 20 years since the early 1900s.

## Duration & Expiration

The current agreement expires **December 31, 2026**. The previous agreement, approved by voters in 2006, was considered groundbreaking at the time for its promises around energy efficiency and renewable development.

## Financial Terms

- Xcel pays Denver approximately **$30 million per year** in franchise fees into the city's General Fund
- Over the current term, Denver saved an estimated **$3 billion** through requirements that Xcel relocate utility lines during public projects
- The proposed new agreement would add **~$10 million annually** from Xcel to bury power lines underground

## What the Agreement Does NOT Cover

Utility rates paid by customers are set exclusively by the **Colorado Public Utilities Commission (PUC)** — they cannot be negotiated through the franchise agreement.

## What Advocates Want Added

Community groups like Together Colorado argue the agreement should go further, even if rates can't be directly lowered. Their demands include:

- Pilot programs and demonstration projects to provide relief to low-income ratepayers
- Better access to rooftop solar and community solar programs
- More detailed data from Xcel on energy use and generation
- Council members Sarah Parady and Shontel Lewis also pushed for greater energy data transparency

## The 2025 Renewal Battle

Mayor Mike Johnston's administration negotiated a renewal agreement and sought to place it on the November 2025 ballot. The Denver City Council blocked it in August 2025, with 7 of 13 members voting against — saying the companion agreement needed more community input and stronger benefits. The nonprofit Together Colorado was credited with driving the surprise defeat, which left city and Xcel officials "stunned."

**Council vote breakdown:**
- **For (6):** Kevin Flynn, Diana Romero Campbell, Amanda Sawyer, Jamie Torres, Darrell Watson, Amanda Sandoval
- **Against (7):** Including swing vote Councilmember Stacie Gilmore, who had criticized Xcel over the failed Swire Coca-Cola plant and Mayor Johnston's modular nuclear power plans

## What Happens If the Agreement Expires?

Xcel could still legally keep operating — the City of Boulder went 10 years without a franchise agreement while attempting to launch a municipal utility. If Denver's agreement expires, the two parties would likely negotiate a temporary extension. However, the city would risk losing the $30M/year in community benefits, and Xcel would face more uncertainty about its right-of-way access. Both sides have strong financial incentives to reach a deal. Xcel has stated there is no threat to local power service.

## What Happens Next

Two paths are on the table:

1. **November 2026 election** — Xcel's preferred path. Tight timeline since PUC must also approve the deal after voters do, and the agreement expires December 31, 2026. Council President Sandoval called this "nerve-wracking."
2. **Special election in June 2026** — Would give more time for PUC review, but would cost an estimated $2–$3 million, which Xcel would be required to pay.

Both sides have committed to renegotiating with broader community and stakeholder engagement heading into 2026.
