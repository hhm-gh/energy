# U.S. Efforts to Reduce Natural Gas Usage

*Research date: July 1, 2026*

---

## What Reductions Are Being Advocated

- **Building electrification mandates**: Over 50 U.S. cities have passed laws discouraging or banning natural gas in new buildings. New York became the first state to ban fossil fuels in most new construction starting in late 2025, while more than 23 other states have passed preemptive laws *blocking* such bans — reflecting a sharp political divide.
- **Displacing gas in power generation**: Rapid solar and battery storage additions in 2025 displaced natural gas-fired electricity generation during many hours of the day, with electric-sector gas use falling 3% that year — a trend advocates want to accelerate by pushing utilities away from gas peaker plants.
- **Reducing methane leakage from infrastructure**: Environmental groups and regulators have pushed for stricter rules on methane leaks from gas wells, pipelines, and distribution networks, arguing that stopping leaks is one of the fastest near-term climate wins available.

---

## Why Advocates Want to Reduce Natural Gas

- **Climate emissions**: U.S. oil and gas consumption produced over 4 billion metric tons of CO₂ in 2019, representing ~80% of energy-related U.S. emissions. Eliminating fossil fuel combustion is seen as essential to meeting mid-century net-zero targets.
- **Methane potency**: Natural gas is primarily methane (CH₄), which is a far more potent short-term greenhouse gas than CO₂. Leakage during extraction, transport, and distribution erodes or eliminates the climate advantage gas has over coal.
- **Health and air quality**: Burning natural gas in homes produces nitrogen dioxide and other pollutants linked to respiratory illness, making indoor gas appliances (stoves, furnaces) a public health concern, not just a climate one.
